20 Laboratory Safety Rules Every Lab Should Follow

A practical laboratory safety checklist covering PPE, labels, chemical handling, ventilation, equipment, waste, emergencies and training.

Written by Hasthas Technical TeamTechnical review: Hasthas Scientific InstrumentsLast reviewed 18 July 2026
Quick answer

Laboratory safety depends on trained people, suitable facilities, clear procedures and properly maintained equipment. This checklist is a general starting point and does not replace a site-specific risk assessment, chemical hygiene plan, biosafety manual or equipment SOP.

20 essential laboratory safety rules

  1. Complete task-specific training before beginning work.
  2. Read the SOP and risk assessment for the procedure.
  3. Wear the required lab coat, gloves, footwear and eye or face protection.
  4. Know the locations of exits, eyewash, safety shower, extinguishers and spill kits.
  5. Label every sample, solution and waste container clearly.
  6. Keep safety data sheets accessible for chemicals in use.
  7. Never eat, drink, apply cosmetics or store food in the laboratory.
  8. Use a chemical fume hood when the risk assessment requires local exhaust.
  9. Use the correct biological safety cabinet for biological containment work.
  10. Do not use a laminar clean bench for hazardous aerosol-generating work.
  11. Inspect glassware and pressure equipment before use.
  12. Keep electrical equipment dry and remove damaged cords from service.
  13. Secure compressed-gas cylinders and use correct regulators.
  14. Segregate chemical, biological, sharps and general waste.
  15. Do not recap, bend or handle sharps unnecessarily.
  16. Balance centrifuge loads and use compatible tubes and rotors.
  17. Use heat-resistant PPE for ovens, furnaces, baths and autoclaves.
  18. Do not bypass alarms, interlocks or safety devices.
  19. Report spills, exposure, damage and near misses immediately.
  20. Leave benches clean, equipment safe and records complete.

Equipment-specific safety

Autoclaves, furnaces, ovens, fume hoods and safety cabinets each have different hazards. Training should cover loading, operation, unloading, emergency shutdown, maintenance and inspection.

Chemical fume hood practices

Keep the sash at the recommended position, avoid blocking airflow, work inside the hood, and do not use the hood as permanent chemical storage. Performance must be checked according to the facility programme.

Emergency preparedness

Emergency instructions should be visible and practised. Staff must know whom to contact, how to isolate the area and when evacuation or medical evaluation is required.

Frequently asked questions

What is the most important laboratory safety rule?

Follow the risk assessment and SOP, use required controls and stop work when conditions are unsafe or unclear.

Is PPE enough to control hazards?

No. Elimination, substitution and engineering controls should be considered before relying on PPE alone.

How often should safety equipment be checked?

Inspection frequency should follow applicable regulations, manufacturer guidance and the facility safety programme.
